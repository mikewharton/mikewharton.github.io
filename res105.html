Modelling chain dynamics

Exploring and modelling the causes and effects of dynamic chain drive vibrations.

This was a six-month research project carried out in a group of five, we went on to win the Bechtel Industrial prize for the highest mark in the cohort. It was following the work of a PhD student whose thesis can be found here. The aim was to help extract performance from bicycle chains, our project fit into this in that we were seeing if it was possible to model the dynamics accurately using a more simple analytical or numerical model. We established a ground truth model (using an existing expensive setup with large motors to spin a bike chain around at a repeatable speed/torque), and then performed experiments on simpler static experimental models, as well as a numerical Finite Element (FE) model, and an analytical model from the mid 20th century by Mahalingam. We managed to track down Mahalingam's original thesis from Sheffield using an inter-library loan which was very interesting. It turned out that the old model was incredibly accurate for predicting resonance, which was remarkable given how computationally cheap it was.

The highlight of this project for me was learning how to use slow motion cameras and lighting, and using this video footage for tracking, similarly to my work on Mean Intensity Mapping (MIM). We also did a few less scientific experiments with the slow motion camera involving balloons when we had some spare time.
